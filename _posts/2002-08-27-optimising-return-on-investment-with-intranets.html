Introduction\n

\n An intranet is a private network that is contained within an enterprise.\n It may consist of many interlinked local area networks and also use\n leased lines in the Wide Area Network. An extranet is also a private\n network, however it shares part of a business's information or operations\n with suppliers, vendors, partners, customers, or other businesses.
\n
\n \n

\n Intranets can serve large international organisations and\n small-to-medium enterprises (MKB). The functionalities within an\n intranet can include information delivery, training, employee\n self-service and human resources. A recent Forrester study that 67% of\n Fortune 1000 companies have an intranet of some form.\n

\n

\n There is no industry standard for the definition of 'Return on\n Investment' (ROI) in eBusiness. A summary of most approaches is ROI a\n measure of money collected to money spent adjusted over time. If you\n spend $12,000 and collected $24,000 over one year the ROI is 100% for\n a single year. More complicated factors such as interest and\n depreciation must also be considered in the equation. The quality of\n the metrics and accounting formulas are critical in developing\n realistic and valuable numbers. Without quality metrics, the exercise\n of determining ROI is pointless.\n

\n

\n ROI is different to cost amortisation. Why? ROI is concerned with\n improving a company's bottom line; not spreading costs over time and\n throughout company divisions.\n

\n

\n Intranet and Extranet ROI can be structured into two hard metrics and\n soft metrics categories. Hard metrics are defined by clear financial\n numbers and arrived at by formulas i.e. €200,000. A soft metric\n is defined as a measurement of 'perceived' benefit i.e. 22%\n improvement in staff attitudes.\n

\n

\n The ownership of ROI figures within an orginisation should be equally\n shared amongst sales, IT and Finance divisions. The result of shared\n ownership has many advantages - realistic numbers, improved delivery\n and better company-wide awareness.\n

\n

\n ROI with intranets and extranets can be hard to determine. Many of\n the benefits fall into the 'soft' category and therefore lack the\n weight of clear revenue improvements. However, there is much evidence\n to suggest the real impact intranets and extranets can have.\n

\n

\n Intranet and Extranet environments can create value for organisations\n in a number of ways. A company can increase sales, decrease costs,\n improve productivity, improve customer service and build intellectual\n capital. Understanding the ROI can inform organisations on the best\n way to invest in their Intranets and Extranets.\n

\n

\n The aim of this paper is to outline how companies improve ROI with\n Intranets and Extranets. The case studies can be a starting point for\n your company to understand its potential ROI with intranets and\n extranets.\n

\n

\n Decreasing Costs\n

\n

\n Intranets and extranets can simply reduce the expense of information\n delivery within an orginisation. Printing and distribution costs can\n reduce significantly via online delivery of information.\n

\n

\n Polymerland Case Study\n

\n

\n Polymerland was formed to offer engineering resins, commodities,\n custom compounds and colorants from the industry's leading\n manufacturers through a network of distribution centers and supporting\n warehouses, strategically located to serve customers around the\n country.\n

\n

\n The first web site to operate for Polymerland was its public web site\n in 1997. This was a simple and static web site. However by 1999 a\n transactional extranet was added. This enabled the connection between\n the company and its customers. Despite initial sales of $10,000 per\n week, sales within two years have reached $50 million to $60 million\n each week - accounting for 60% of company orders.\n

\n

\n Developing a new sales channel really had impact on the bottom-line.\n The customer service division of Polymerland has reduced costs by 35%\n and the company receives 200,000 fewer telephone calls from its\n customers.\n

\n

\n Cisco Systems Case Study\n

\n

\n Cisco Systems, Inc. is the worldwide leader in networking for the\n Internet. Cisco's Internet Protocol-based (IP) networking solutions\n are the foundation of the Internet and most corporate, education, and\n government networks around the world. Cisco provides the broadest line\n of solutions for transporting data, voice and video within buildings,\n across campuses, or around the world.\n

\n

\n Cisco successfully implemented an employee self-service tool called\n METRO. This employee tool enabled cost reduction but also improved\n efficiency. All employees can submit their personal business expenses\n via the intranet Cisco's METRO expense reporting application has\n reduced the cost of processing expense reports (compared to the\n traditional paper system) from US$50.69 in 1996 to $1.90 in 1999.\n

\n

\n Beautiful in its simplicity and powerful in its benefits. METRO\n translates into a 96% reduction in costs or US$77 million annually.\n Also gained was the average processing time of expenses was reduced\n from 21 days to only 4 days.\n

\n

\n For information regarding Cisco visit http://www.cisco.com\n

\n Productivity Increases\n

\n

\n Information is an important tool in everyday business. The\n company-wide access to information can determine the success of sales,\n time-to-market, customer service and many other mission critical\n operations. Intranets and Extranets can, when built with the users\n needs in mind, improve this information delivery process. Productivity\n can also be improved with faster and more efficient processing of\n transactions and orders.\n

\n Mentor Graphics Case Study\n

\n

\n Mentor Graphics® is a technology leader in electronic design\n automation (EDA), providing software and hardware design solutions\n that enable companies to send better electronic products to market\n faster and more cost-effectively. The company has 3,500 employees,\n Revenues approximately $600 million in 2001 and 66 locations\n worldwide.\n

\n

\n Solving high tech problems is a demanding task for the customer\n engineers at Mentor. In the old days the quality and efficiency of\n customer service was dependant on the memory of any given engineer.\n

\n

\n Customer Service Improvement\n

\n

\n Many facets of customer service are dependent on face-to-face\n contact. This can never be replaced by any technology. However, there\n are components of the customer relationship than can profit from\n optimisation via intranets and extranets. The benefit of\n extranet-based customer service can be mutually shared between both\n the end customer and the company.\n

\n NOL Group Case Study\n

\n

\n The NOL Group today provides services in more than 100 countries and\n operates one of the largest containership and Aframax tanker fleets\n worldwide. It is also the largest shipping company listed on the\n Singapore Exchange with a market capitalization of S$1.24 billion as\n of 7 June 2002.\n

\n

\n In an effort to avoid large reproduction of paper work for their\n clients, NOL moved a number of customer transactions online. These\n include electronic bills of shipping, online versions of the letters\n of credit required for insurance, and real-time rate quotes for\n specialized cargoes.\n

\n

\n Currently 38% of APL's customers conduct their business via online\n transactions, up from 8% in 1999. Amazingly 25% of APL's North\n American customers never see a paper bill of shipping. Over the past\n two years volume of shipments has increased by nearly 10%.\n Significantly a 20% reduction in the cost of processing bills of\n shipping has occurred at the same time.\n

\n

\n With the benefits passed on to customers, NOL Group enjoys more\n efficiency and more profits and happy customers.\n

\n

\n For more information on NOL Group visit \n http://www.nol.com.sg\n

\n Mary Kay Case Study\n

\n

\n Mark Kay is a manufacturer of beauty products in the USA. Revenues for\n 2001 were $2.6 billion.\n

\n

\n Rather than casuing a channel conflict, Mark Kay decided to use the\n Internet as a medium to support is sales representatives. By helping\n its beauty consultants set up and improve their business the company\n believed it could increase sales.\n

\n

\n Anecdotes from the sales professionals showed how the intranet\n enabled them to distribute sales information quickly to their\n customers via email and faster sales cycles followed.\n

\n

\n The total investment to date is $15 million during the past five\n years. 24% of the sales force have their own web page and use the\n intranet regularly. The sales force also now orders 70% of Mary Kay's\n revenue online. Most importantly, huge efficiencies have been created.\n The cost per order in the company used to be over $3, now it's below\n $1 due to the intranet.\n

\n

\n For more information about Mark Kay visit http://www.marykay.com/\n

\n Summary\n

\n

\n Intranets and Extranets have undoubted impact on a company's bottom\n line. From delivering simple contact information to sophisticated\n group purchasing the evidence is clear.\n

\n

\n However, companies must vigorously understand their business and the\n manner in which eBusiness initiatives can benefit them. Many companies\n have unique situations that require tailored solutions that really\n integrate into the orginistaion. Many companies do not discover what\n users goals are. Therefore, the final solution does not generate the\n desired results.\n

\n

\n The choice of technology has often been a leading factor within an\n intranet or extranet. Issues such as security, compatibility and\n vendor relationships often are considered when the full facts are not\n known. How can a company make a technology/functionality decision on\n an intranet without first determining what the users need are and the\n financial return for solving this need?\n

\n

\n An informed decision based on the business, user and technology goals\n will balance the key disciplines within eBusiness. An optimisied ROI\n is understanding of the disciplines with eBusiness and based on sound\n metrics and formulas.\n
