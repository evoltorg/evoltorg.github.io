\nFor those who use characters in their copy that don't normally appear on the keyboard, it's always been a hit-and-miss game of tracking down the ISO character entity and choosing between the named and numeric value. In fact, so many books on HTML, as well as online resources, have provided the wrong entities for so long, few knew it until the W3C validator started throwing them back as errors. You can't imagine how many em-dashes I had to find and convert from &#151; to the correct &#8212;.\n

\nThe W3C character entity reference is certainly definitive, but not practical. It offers no display of how these entities might be rendered, leaving you to copy and paste based on descriptions until you find the right one.\n

\nWell, I got sick of guessing, so I took the W3C documentation and turned it into a handy chart for my staff. Now it sits on our intranet for all to enjoy. Oh... and here... now... as well.\n

\nFor those who might not normally use character entities, or who've never heard of The Elements of Typographic Style, you might not have been exposed to reasons why you would even care about characters not on your keyboard. I'd suggest you surf over to the A List Apart article The Trouble With EM 'n EN.\n

\nBy the way, if you need to know what browsers support what character entities, just view this page and look for the characters in the tables. One column shows the character as called by its numeric entity, and one shows it as called by its named entity. This is the chart for HTML 4.x, but also applies to XHTML 1.x.\n

Character entity references in HTML 4

\nThe charts below will allow you to copy and paste the appropriate character and numeric entities for your documents. To be sure a particular browser supports the entities (both named and numeric), simply open your browser to this pages and view the charts. If the character you want doesn't appear in the target browser, it doesn't work (simple, huh?).\n

\nWhat you'll find below is the copy of the character entity specification from the W3C with my tabled versions of the entities interspersed.\n

24.1 Introduction to character entity references

A character entity reference is an SGML\nconstruct that references a character of the document\ncharacter set.

\nHTML 4.x, as well as XHTML 1.x, supports several sets of character entity\nreferences:

    \n
  • ISO 8859-1 (Latin-1) characters In accordance with\nsection 14 of [RFC1866], the set of Latin-1 entities has been extended by\nthis specification to cover the whole right part of ISO-8859-1 (all code\npositions with the high-order bit set), including the already commonly used &nbsp;, © and ®. The names of the entities are taken from\nthe appendices of SGML (defined in [ISO8879]).

  • symbols, mathematical symbols, and Greek letters. These\ncharacters may be represented by glyphs in the Adobe font "Symbol".

  • markup-significant and internationalization characters\n(e.g., for bidirectional text).
    • \n

The following sections present the complete lists of character entity\nreferences.

\n

ISO 8859-1

24.2 Character entity references for ISO 8859-1 characters

The character entity references in this section produce characters whose\nnumeric equivalents should already be supported by conforming HTML 2.0 user\nagents. Thus, the character entity reference &divide; is a more convenient\nform than &#247; for obtaining the division sign (÷).

To support these named entities, user agents need only recognize the entity\nnames and convert them to characters that lie within the repertoire of \n[ISO88591].

Character 65533 (FFFD hexadecimal) is the last valid character in UCS-2.\n65534 (FFFE hexadecimal) is unassigned and reserved as the byte-swapped version\nof ZERO WIDTH NON-BREAKING SPACE for byte-order detection purposes. 65535 (FFFF\nhexadecimal) is unassigned.

Symbols, Mathematical Symbols, and Greek Letters

24.3 Character entity references for\nsymbols, mathematical symbols, and Greek letters

The character entity references in this section produce characters that may\nbe represented by glyphs in the widely available Adobe Symbol font, including\nGreek characters, various bracketing symbols, and a selection of mathematical\noperators such as gradient, product, and summation symbols.

To support these entities, user agents may support full [ISO10646] or use\nother means. Display of glyphs for these characters may be obtained by being\nable to display the relevant [ISO10646] characters or\nby other means, such as internally mapping the listed entities, numeric\ncharacter references, and characters to the appropriate position in some font\nthat contains the requisite glyphs.

\n

When to use Greek entities. This entity set contains\nall the letters used in modern Greek. However, it does not include Greek\npunctuation, precomposed accented characters nor the non-spacing accents\n(tonos, dialytika) required to compose them. There are no archaic letters,\nCoptic-unique letters, or precomposed letters for Polytonic Greek. The entities\ndefined here are not intended for the representation of modern Greek text and\nwould not be an efficient representation; rather, they are intended for\noccasional Greek letters used in technical and mathematical works.

\n

Markup-Significant and Internationalization Characters

24.4 Character entity references for\nmarkup-significant and internationalization characters

The character entity references in this section are for escaping\nmarkup-significant characters (these are the same as those in HTML 2.0 and\n3.2), for denoting spaces and dashes. Other characters in this section apply to\ninternationalization issues such as the disambiguation of bidirectional text\n(see the section on bidirectional\ntext for details).

Entities have also been added for the remaining characters occurring in\nCP-1252 which do not occur in the HTMLlat1 or HTMLsymbol entity sets. These all\noccur in the 128 to 159 range within the CP-1252 charset. These entities permit\nthe characters to be denoted in a platform-independent manner.

To support these entities, user agents may support full [ISO10646] or use\nother means. Display of glyphs for these characters may be obtained by being\nable to display the relevant [ISO10646] characters or\nby other means, such as internally mapping the listed entities, numeric\ncharacter references, and characters to the appropriate position in some font\nthat contains the requisite glyphs.
