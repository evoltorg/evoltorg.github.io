An Overview of Personas

\n

One of the most important things you can do to help make your site more usable\n is to get to know your users. This can be done in various ways. Conducting\n usability tests (gorilla or more in-depth tests), soliciting user feedback,\n interviewing and surveying are all good ways of doing this, and there are many\n more. Once you get started, you will have a pretty good basis of information\n on your users to help you design and advocate for them.

\n

You can further increase the usefulness of this information, as well as add\n to it, by creating personas for your users.

\n

A persona is a user profile that you can use to help make design decisions,\n as well as use to aid you in other ways. These profiles are created from your\n knowledge of your users, usually knowledge gained from user testing and research.\n Think of it as having a “virtual” user to bounce ideas off and\n help you keep the goals of the user in mind on a day-to-day basis. They are\n another powerful and valuable tool you can add to your toolbox.

\n

What I’ll do here is show you a quick and easy method to help\n you create your personas, as well as the basics of how they can be used. This\n is by no means going to be the only way this can be done, nor is it the only\n way personas can be used. There is much more to the world of personas than\n I’ll be discussing here.

\n

The more you put into your personas (and any other usability strategy) the more you’ll\n get out of them – I just hope to get you started. To that end I’ll\n give you some links to resources that will explain in much more detail how\n you can use, create and learn from personas when we’re done.

\n

Start with Research.

\n

The first and most important thing you’ll need to do is gather information\n about your users. Depending on your resources and budget this can be done in\n various ways and to varying levels of detail. Let’s concentrate on a\n few simple ways that will specifically help you create personas.

\n

One of the best ways you can get good information for your personas is by\n interviewing your users one on one. If you are planning on doing some usability\n testing, add ten minutes or so to users’ sessions and have someone\n sit down and ask them some questions. If you can’t get in direct contact\n with them, provide an survey (by email for example) for them to fill out\n and send to you.

\n

It’s fairly important to get a decent sized sample of user data. What\n you’ll need to look for when building your personas is patterns or similarities\n between users. You’ll want to avoid keying in on one particular person\n for a persona.

\n

Things you’ll want to find out about:

\n
    \n
  • Personal information, such as age, gender and location.
    • \n
  • Technical information\n like what kind of computer and browser they use, how and why they use the\n Web, and how often.
    • \n
  • Their relationship is to your company, client or organization.
    • \n
  • How they\n view your site, or potential site, as well as those of your competitors.
    • \n
  • What they like\n in a Web site and what they don’t.
    • \n
\n

You don’t need to limit it to that, and this list should vary depending\n on the type of site you are building, your audiences and the business you are\n in. For example, I work for a children’s hospital, so when interviewing\n parents we asked some generic questions about their kids and their relationship\n with the hospital. All of that was very important when building our personas.

\n

In addition to the interview data, you can gather some demographic type data\n from your server logs (technical), and by talking to stakeholders. A good group\n to speak with, as they will most likely have some of this, is your marketing\n department.

\n

There are other ways to get information about your users that would be helpful\n in persona creation. Focus groups, emotional response testing, site feedback\n forms and surveys can all help you out.

\n

Once you have gathered your data, and hopefully you will have enough to begin\n to see some patterns emerge, you’ll need to sort that data and summarize\n it for review. One thing you’ll want to keep in mind is to separate the\n patterns so that things that apply to the majority of users are not lumped\n in with patterns specific to single users or very few users. This way you’ll\n be able to identify a common or primary user base (and persona) as well as\n a secondary persona. More on that shortly.

\n

It’s alive! Time to Create.

\n

Now that you have gathered and grouped your data it’s time to create\n a few personas. There are many ways you can do this, all of them good. What\n I have personally found easiest is to create the personas myself, and not involve\n a group. This may not work well for you, however. Feel free to involve others\n if you need to.

\n

I start with my raw data, and I take the groupings I’ve come up with\n and try to form that data into the traits of a person. I do this until my data\n is exhausted, or as close as I can get to that. This process will vary depending\n on the amount of data you have as well as the variety. Hopefully you will have\n a pretty decent understanding of your audiences and users through the process\n of gathering the data that will allow you to make some assumptions. Another\n way you can help yourself is try to find a few “real” people in\n your pool of data and model the personas after them. Do change the names to\n protect the innocent.

\n

Once you have some personas mapped out with user data you can identify your\n primary and secondary user groups. Pretty much all that means is you’ll\n want to take the two personas that best describe who you think your largest\n user group and a secondary user group that is very different from the first\n but still very important.

\n

At this point you should have a persona to represent the most common user\n group, a secondary persona to represent another vastly different group as well\n as a few other personas to fill in the rest of your user groups. You don’t\n want too many, so if you have quite a few, or any that overlap, either combine\n them or cut them.

\n

If you like you can also get creative and create a back-story or add some\n personality to your personas. This can not only be fun, but also help you later\n on when working on real world problems.

\n

Put Your Personas to Work

\n

So now that you have personas, what do you do? Well, that is up to you and\n the needs of you and your team members. I use my personas for many things.\n Here’s a list to get you started thinking about how you (and others you\n work with) can use your personas:

\n
    \n
  • For surrogate user testing.
    • \n
  • To help advocate for your users.
    • \n
  • To communicate user needs.
    • \n
  • To evaluate new features.
    • \n
  • To help make design decisions.
    • \n
  • To help weigh business decisions.
    • \n
  • For task analysis and use cases.
    • \n
  • For customer service scripts.
    • \n
\n

There is a certain amount of role playing and make-believe involved when putting\n your personas into practice. However silly it may seem, just having the personas\n around will help you not only think more user- and customer- centric, but help\n communicate the wants and needs of your users to anyone who might not understand\n how those needs and wants can help everyone involved.

\n

There is a whole lot more to personas than what I’ve talked about here.\n I’ve found them to be a very helpful and very interesting design tool,\n but that is only the beginning. If you want to learn more about personas here\n are some great resources. Some of these were instrumental in helping me get\n my first personas created.
