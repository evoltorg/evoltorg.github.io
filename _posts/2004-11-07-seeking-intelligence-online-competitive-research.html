Finding online information\nabout your competitors is easy - the difficult part is finding meaning.\nWhen researching the competition, you can avoid pitfalls by seeking\n'intelligence' instead of 'information'.

\n

The difference between\n'information' and 'intelligence' is twofold: purpose and process.\nHaving a purpose and a process for gathering information ensures that\nthe results will be useful - instead of merely informative.

\n

A well-defined purpose allows\nyou to search the Internet broadly and deeply without drowning\nin a flood of marginal information.

\n

The process for gathering good\ncompetitive intelligence is straightforward: identify industry/market\ntrends and competitors; define your questions; find the answers;\nanalyze the results; and act on them.

\n

The process begins with a wide\nsearch of the Internet to define trends in your industry/market and\ncompetitors.

\n

Useful\nresources include search engines, newspapers, business journals, and\ntrade press and online newsletters. Consider resources such as NewsIsFree\nand other topical news 'clipping' sites. PR\nNewswire and Business\nWire can be useful sources for\nnews releases and competitive PR.

\n

Beginning with a broad\nindustry/market search decreases the likelihood that you'll overlook\npotential competitors and non-competing businesses in the industry that\nmay pose as sources of benchmarking, market insight and inspiration.

\n

Once you've identified trends\nand competitors, you can identify the questions you want to answer\nabout your competitors. Finding competitors' identities, pricing,\nplans, strengths, weaknesses, suppliers and customers play a very\nimportant part in formulating an effective business strategy.

\n

Finding answers often begin at\na competitor's own web site. When visiting competitors' web sites look\nbeyond product/service information. You can also learn by viewing a\ncompetitor's employment opportunities, organization chart, supplier and\nvendor lists, and press releases.

\n
    \n
  • Explore\ntheir news/press area.\nOften, you can download their press kit or add your personal email\naddress to their email subscription list.
    • \n
  • Search engines make it easy\nto\nfind PowerPoint presentations, speeches and white papers. Use search\nterms such as .ppt,\n .doc\nand .pdf
    • \n
  • Topics such as Investor\nRelations often include annual reports (even for private companies).
    • \n
  • Forms that ask "How did you\nhear about us?" provide a list of locations where the company is\nspending marketing dollars.
    • \n
\n

After you've explored a\ncompetitor's web site, use other web sites to learn about their\norganization. Conduct keyword searches on search engines, public\nrecords sites, discussion groups and blogs.

\n

Notable\nsites:\n

\n
    \n
  • WayBack\nMachine
    \nAn Internet archive. Use this site to see historical versions of the\ncompany's web site. It will help determine changes in branding and\nofferings.
    • \n
  • F***edCompany.com
    \nA site featuring company rumors and internal memos.
    • \n
  • Alexa
    \nObtain traffic information about the traffic ranking, page views, and\nreach of a competitor's web site.
    • \n
  • US\nPatent & Trademark Office
    \nConduct a basic patent search on competitors
    • \n
\n

These\nrepresent only a handful\nof sites that are useful for gathering competitive intelligence. Based\non your industry, competitors, questions and creativity, it is possible\nto find a wide selection of useful online/offline resources.

\n

According to the Society\nof Competitive Intelligence Professionals,\nmore than 95% of information that is required for gathering competitive\nintelligence is publicly available from open sources.\n
