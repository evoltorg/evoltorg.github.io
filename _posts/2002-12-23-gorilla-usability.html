\n Do You Know Your Users?\n

\n

\n That is a tough question to answer. It could mean so many different\n things. That's fairly obvious. But it's a question everyone who works\n with technology that other people use should ask themselves. I've\n often asked myself this question, and have come up with different\n answers at different times on different projects. I came to the\n realization at some point that for the most part I knew who my users\n were, and I had an idea of how they used my sites, but other than that\n all I had was a bunch of data and even more assumptions.\n

\n

\n I've been lucky enough to be involved in some formal usability studies\n before, and it was an informative, eye-opening experience. The main\n problem with these formal studies is that they are expensive. They are\n also arcane and time consuming. In my case we hired a company to come\n in, set up studies, conduct them and hand over the results at the end.\n I was able to weasel my way into these sessions as an observer and\n that was much more informative to me as one who actually works on the\n site than all of the statistics and reports combined.\n

\n

\n I couldn't help but think, "Hey, I could do that." I'm the guy who\n actually designs and builds the sites, why not cut out the middleman?\n

\n

\n That's what this article is all about. I'm going to show you some ways\n to get to know your users in a real world, out-from-behind-the-glass\n kind of way. There is a place for formal usability and if you have the\n opportunity (read: time and money) to do them, I recommend doing so.\n It's also great to learn from what others have experienced, to read\n books on usability and to keep up with the gurus. I've done all of\n those and frankly they all pale in comparison to the information I get\n from, what I like to call, "Gorilla Usability."\n

\n

\n What Is Gorilla Usability?\n

\n

\n Don't worry. I know how to spell. I imagine you've heard the term\n "guerilla marketing"? Well, it's kind of like that, but a lot less\n subtle. It's about getting out from behind the video camera, the\n reports, the stats and all the guru commandments and actually getting\n to know your users. It's about making that direct connection between\n the makers and the users. It's about getting into their world, seeing\n how they use your sites on their turf. It's about learning how to\n listen to what your users want, and getting past your own assumptions.\n

\n

\n There are many benefits to getting to know your users. Some are\n obvious and some are not so clear. If you make an effort to understand\n your users on every project you are involved in you will begin to be\n able to make design decisions that will save you time and grief down\n the road. Your sites will be better tailored to user wants and needs\n and in the long run you'll save your clients and organizations money\n as well as help to build a solid relationship with the people who\n matter most.\n

\n

\n Quite a bit of what I'm going to talk about it common sense, and\n really there are many directions you could go with this. The main\n thing is to make a commitment to get to know your users, doing\n something about that commitment and then following up with all the\n great user feedback and observations you'll have.\n

\n

\n So, How Do I Start?\n

\n

\n The hardest part about Gorilla Usability is getting that initial\n contact with your users. I've worked on many projects where the Web\n team is working in a vacuum, with little or no contact with the users\n aside from Web stats logs and the occasional e-mail. Depending on the\n type of project you are working on you have a few options here, and\n I'd stress that a few users are better than none (but none can be\n better than one or two — more on that later), so if you can put your\n designs in front of family members, friends or co-workers it's better\n than not trying to get user feedback at all.\n

\n

\n If you are working on a redesign or project that has another related\n site with existing users this will be easiest. There are a few ways to\n get some of your users to help out and most of those are free — or\n really cheap. It could be as simple as posting a link on your site, or\n sending an e-mail to your mailing list asking for participation in\n user studies. Make it clear that you are doing this to help them, and\n let them know you need their help to make a better experience for\n everyone. If you have a small user base or worse, apathetic users,\n offer a small gift or to feed them, that seems to work well. In my\n experience there are always a few people who are willing to help out\n and share their opinions with you. These folks are usually more\n engaged than the "typical users" that you'd get for a formal study -\n and they're usually free!\n

\n

\n Another method is a simple feedback form, or survey posted on your\n site. This works really well for Intranets or projects with highly\n engaged users. It's a great way to collect some basic user data and\n opinions and if you make a request for volunteers you'll quickly have\n a user base with which to set up you studies.\n

\n

\n We've Got Volunteers. Now What?\n

\n

\n Now comes the most tedious part. You'll need to set up appointments\n with your volunteers. This can be time consuming and frustrating at\n times, but hang in there. I've done this a few times now and it's gone\n fairly smoothly, I found that making sure they knew that you were\n going the extra mile and advocating for them seemed to help this\n along. You can have them come to you, or, what I'd suggest — you can\n go to them.\n

\n

\n Conducting the study at a user's home, desk or office will give you a\n greater insight into how they use your sites, and the Web in general.\n It'll be an extra effort on your part, but the payoff is great,\n believe me. You'll see things you'd have never seen in a classroom\n setting or at your own workstation. I recently did a study where we\n wanted to test a PDF form. We knew there were some problems with it,\n but we didn't have a handle of how people used this form on the Web.\n The idea was to have people fill it out online, save, print and then\n fax it in. Well, I went out to conduct the study at a few of our\n user's offices and I found out, to my complete surprise, that most of\n them didn't have a printer connected to the computer they used to hit\n the Web. They had a copy of the form and when they ran out they used\n the copy machine to make more. One office had only a small laptop\n connected directly to a DSL line. It was shared by five people, no\n network, no printer, nothing. And this was closer to our typical user\n than we'd ever dreamed.\n

\n

\n One thing to remember when setting up these studies is that if you\n have several people on your Web team conduct studies — which I think\n can be a really good idea, especially for small teams — you need to\n make sure that each person gets at least three users per study. If\n possible it would be good to do six or more. The problem with doing\n one or two users is that you tend to identify with those users and\n when it comes time to get your observations together and make a plan\n on improving your site you'll have a tough time seeing the big\n picture. Every user has particular problems, and some of these might\n be limited to that one user. You need to know when to let those go and\n take care of the issues that matter to the majority. In my experience\n it takes at least three users to begin to see patterns emerge.\n

\n

\n Now Comes The Fun Part\n

\n

\n You've got volunteers lined up, you've scheduled appointments to meet\n with them in their lairs, now what? You need a plan. It's Gorilla\n Usability, but it's still smart usability. What I like to do before I\n go out to conduct a study is write up a simple "to do" list for me and\n a simple script for the user. I put to paper what I want to tell the\n user and how I'd like to set up the study. I do this in a very loose\n manner, as these studies, unlike formal usability studies, tend to\n have a mind of their own and often go all over the place. That's a\n good thing in my mind, it keeps it interesting, makes it fun and may\n uncover some things about your users you might not have gotten to, but\n I at least try to have some semblance of order to them.\n

\n

\n The "to do" list is something like this:\n

\n
    \n
  • \nThank the user for helping out, let them know what the study is\nabout, and that we are testing the site and not the user. Let them\nknow it's OK to make a wrong turn and that all their feedback will\nhelp make a better user experience.\n
    • \n
  • \nAsk the user what they think the site is about, or if they use it\ncurrently what they use it for. Ask them what they like and don't\nlike and just get a general feel for how they view the site.\n
    • \n
  • \nHave them look at the site as a whole. Ask what they think might be\nin different areas of the site, or what different functions would\nbe for. Gauge their expectations and first impressions.\n
    • \n
  • \nNow give the users some tasks. Use a script based on some areas you\nwould like feedback on and some other general areas or functions.\nKeep the user on track if possible, but note where they deviate.\nTake notes, observe and encourage the user to keep talking about\nwhat they are thinking. Try to avoid coaching unless the user asks\nfor help.\n
    • \n
  • \nWhen the tasks are done, thank the user for their help.\n
    • \n
  • \nAsk the user for their general opinion and impression of the site\nand the experience. Let them express how they felt while using the\nsite.\n
    • \n
  • \nEnd the study. Make sure you give them your contact information in\ncase they have any more feedback.\n
    • \n
\n

\n That is just an example, your plan could be more or less detailed.\n It's just a road map for the study to help keep you focused. The next\n thing you'll want to prepare is a script for the user tasks. Pretty\n much what you want is some tasks for the user to do that will help\n simulate a typical session using your site. This will vary greatly\n depending on your project. For example, on a corporate Intranet you\n might have a script like this:\n

\n
    \n
  • \nFrom the home page find Human Resources\n
    • \n
  • \nNow locate the cafeteria menu\n
    • \n
  • \nGo back to the home page\n
    • \n
  • \nSearch for information on the president of the company\n
    • \n
  • \nLocate the casual Friday policy\n
    • \n
  • \nSubmit a classified ad\n
    • \n
  • \nFind the holiday schedule\n
    • \n
\n

\n You get the idea. The trick here is to give them tasks to do without\n telling them how to do them. You'd say "Find the weather in Sydney"\n rather than, "Go to the Weather page, choose Australia, then select\n Sydney from the drop down menu." This way you can observe how the user\n gets to that information. Lots of times they do exactly opposite of\n what you'd think.\n

\n

\n Once you've conducted the study and have lots of notes and\n observations your almost ready to start advocating for your users and\n solving some of their problems.\n

\n

\n Almost Ready?\n

\n

\n Well, yeah. Before I go on I wanted to let you know that there are a\n few other ways you can get some good data about your users aside from\n the studies. I've already mentioned having surveys and feedback forms\n on your site as a good way to get volunteers to help out with your\n studies. These are also nice things to have when collecting data to\n help you move forward solving issues.\n

\n

\n Another great way to help understand your users is by looking into\n your Web logs. This way you can get some data on what pages are used\n the most, where users are entering and leaving the site and lots of\n other interesting data. Alone this data is nice to have, but coupled\n with the feedback and usability study observation it becomes a\n powerful ally in helping you determine what issues to tackle first.\n

\n

\n There are many different software packages out there that can help you\n decipher these logs and put them into a format that you can read and\n use. One I particularly like is ClickTracks It takes your logs and\n couples them with the actual display of your Web site, showing you\n visually where and how often people click different links.\n

\n

\n There are lots more ways to connect with your users, so be creative.\n

\n

\n Get to Work\n

\n

\n Now you're ready to get to making your site a better place for your\n users. In my experience there are always a few obvious things you can\n take care of right away. If your logs are telling you that 60% of your\n users hit the menu on your restaurant site, but you moved the link to\n it with the new design and now people can't find it - you might want\n to fix that. What I do is go through all the data I have and start to\n identify patterns and similarities. Some are fairly obvious and some\n harder to find.\n

\n

\n Once we have these it's time to work out a plan for getting them taken\n care of, we've learned about our users needs and are ready to advocate\n for them in our project plans. We also have a basis from which we can\n make design decisions and another stakeholder to refer to when\n weighing options.\n

\n

\n It's also a good idea to keep the lines of communication open. Sites\n tend to evolve, business rules change, user bases change, heck,\n everything changes, and usability needs to be an ongoing process. Once\n you're done you should start thinking about the next round.\n

\n

\n I can say that these types of studies and just the effort of getting\n to know the users of my sites has made me a better designer, a more\n efficient developer and has saved me time and effort in the long run.\n Not to mention enhancing the experience of people who use my sites.\n There is a lot more to usability that this — all of it good stuff —\n but I like to boil usability down to one simple rule. Get to know your\n users. How you do that is up to you.\n
